The Assumption of Nalantei

by Stephen Marvin

Part II - Whispers in Laughter (October)

(Page 11 of 16)

In a land of unfamiliar footsteps, Nalantei spoke to her new sister, Naperu, the first wife of her husband, and rejoiced in her kindness. The shape of the older woman's hut was familiar too, made so by the common accouterments of a woman's home, much like Sienti's, where the ornament of goat skins — draped like a veil against the rain, appearing merely dusty grey in idleness — and the necessary presentations and fortifications, the nkarau of new sweet goat milk, a pot of fat, palm fiber baskets to be carried by donkeys or one of the few camels in the settlement, a kudu horn and the sleeping mat, were always prepared and ordered for the man, Sopoitan, who might come to Naperu, the first, to lie with her.

The sky was clear as the women passed by like clouds, like a dream until Nalantei spoke to them, their direction not hers until she chose to detain them from their path. Most of them, like herself, were new to this settlement, not born Lngwesi, not born with Nkanyit instilled so deeply, but married into custom, and conjoined.

This was Nalantei's home and her land, the locus of her new life, as she came to understand it must be.

As Nalantei's gaze passed from the eyes of her friend, she who would be her sister, the first wife of Sopoitan (though in age more like a mother), she admired the tight weave of the new hut, the doum palm not yet grey from time, dust or water's depredations, and thought that she would soon begin to build her own home, perhaps near to this hut which she had helped Naperu to build. Hers would be on the same plan, but in her own style. And in the moon's second fullness she would not reside with Naperu, or join her daughter, Maluni — who talked and laughed long into the night — but as a new wife, Nalantei would receive her husband, and the women would accept her fully among them; she would have her indomitable place, her own shield against the sun, the rain, and her man, Sopoitan, and the other, Lekiso.

Her sister's smile was on her again; it seemed somehow that those eyes — or was it the turn of her head and the movement of her lips — were old and familiar, though in gentleness, more feminine than her mother, and though graceful, unlike her father's generous furtive smile. It seemed to her that she could no longer remember exactly how her mother turned her head, or even if it was her mother she was remembering.
